Six Principles for Working With Emotions

A Guide to Emotional Healing

Home Blog

By Meli Pil, LMHC | January 4, 2026

Emotions are not the enemy—they are wise messengers guiding us toward growth and healing. In Emotion-Focused Therapy and related approaches, we learn to work with emotions rather than against them. Here are six essential principles for healthy emotional processing:

  1. Awareness: Instead of being overwhelmed or fused with the emotion ("I am angry"), step back and observe it ("I notice anger arising in my body"). This mindful awareness creates space, reduces reactivity, and allows you to respond rather than react.
  2. Expression: Emotions need a voice. Allow yourself to name, verbalize, or symbolically express the feeling (through words, art, movement). Important note: Secondary emotions (like defensiveness or surface anger) often mask primary ones (like hurt or fear). Expressing only secondary emotions keeps the cycle going—true healing requires reaching and expressing the underlying primary emotion.
  3. Regulation: When emotions feel overwhelming, gently bring down their intensity so they become manageable. Use grounding techniques (deep breathing, body awareness, safe place imagery) to create a window of tolerance where deeper processing can occur.
  4. Reflection: Once the emotion is at a tolerable level, explore its meaning. What is this feeling telling me about my needs, values, or past experiences? Reflection helps uncover patterns and turns raw emotion into insight.
  5. Transforming Emotion: Emotions can shift when met with new energy. For example, shame (often rooted in self-attack) can transform into healthy anger or assertiveness. In Internal Family Systems (IFS) therapy, connecting with "Self-energy"—the calm, compassionate, curious core of who you are—naturally softens and transforms painful emotions.
  6. Corrective Emotional Experience: The most powerful healing often happens interpersonally. Experiencing a new, caring, attuned connection (in therapy or safe relationships) rewires old emotional patterns, replacing isolation or fear with felt safety and understanding.
