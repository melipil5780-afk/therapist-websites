Unlocking the Source of Darkness: What Really Causes Sadness, Anxiety, Shame, Confusion, Emptiness, and Numbness?

By Meli Pil, LMHC | December 31, 2024

This article dives deep into the hidden roots of emotional chaos, exposing what really fuels the inner storm that goes beyond life’s everyday challenges. The emotional pain we experience—sadness, anxiety, shame, confusion, emptiness, and numbness—is not random. It is the result of unmet emotional needs, deeply ingrained schemas, and maladaptive coping modes that develop over time. Understanding these core elements is essential for anyone seeking lasting relief and healing.

5 Essential Emotional Needs

Every human being has five core emotional needs. When these needs are consistently unmet in childhood, they create vulnerabilities that manifest as emotional pain in adulthood.

  1. Safety and Security: The need to feel safe, protected, and stable. Without this, individuals may develop anxiety or hypervigilance.
  2. Autonomy, Competence, and Identity: The need to feel independent, capable, and have a clear sense of self. When unmet, it can lead to confusion or identity issues.
  3. Freedom to Express Valid Needs and Emotions: The need to express feelings and needs without fear of rejection or punishment. Suppression here leads to shame or numbness.
  4. Spontaneity, Play, and Limits: The need for joy, fun, and healthy boundaries. Lack of this can cause emptiness or chronic boredom.
  5. Realistic Limits and Self-Control: The need for reasonable limits to guide behavior. Without it, individuals may struggle with impulsivity or excessive guilt.

Four Ways Unmet Needs Lead to Negative Schemas

Unmet needs create negative schemas—deeply held beliefs about ourselves, others, and the world. These schemas operate unconsciously and drive emotional pain.

  1. Direct Trauma: Severe abuse or neglect directly creates schemas like defectiveness or mistrust.
  2. Frustration of Core Needs: Chronic frustration of needs (e.g., lack of affection) leads to schemas like emotional deprivation.
  3. Internalization of Parental Voice: Children internalize critical or demanding parental voices, creating schemas like unrelenting standards or punitiveness.
  4. Modeling: Children imitate parental behaviors and attitudes, adopting schemas like self-sacrifice or subjugation.

18 Maladaptive Schemas

  • Abandonment/Instability: Belief that loved ones will leave or die.
  • Mistrust/Abuse: Expectation that others will harm, abuse, or betray.
  • Emotional Deprivation: Belief that needs for love, understanding, or support will never be met.
  • Defectiveness/Shame: Feeling inherently flawed, unworthy, or unlovable.
  • Social Isolation/Alienation: Feeling different from others or not belonging.
  • Dependence/Incompetence: Belief that one cannot handle daily responsibilities without help.
  • Vulnerability to Harm or Illness: Fear of catastrophe (illness, disaster) without protection.
  • Enmeshment/Undeveloped Self: Excessive emotional involvement with parents, leading to underdeveloped identity.
  • Failure: Belief that one is fundamentally inadequate and will fail.
  • Entitlement/Grandiosity: Belief that one is superior and entitled to special treatment.
  • Insufficient Self-Control/Self-Discipline: Difficulty maintaining goals or controlling impulses.
  • Subjugation: Feeling forced to surrender control to others to avoid harm or rejection.
  • Self-Sacrifice: Excessive focus on meeting others' needs at the expense of one's own.
  • Approval-Seeking/Recognition-Seeking: Excessive need for approval or recognition.
  • Negativity/Pessimism: Focus on negative aspects of life, expecting the worst.
  • Inhibition: Suppression of emotions, spontaneity, and joy to avoid shame or disapproval.
  • Unrelenting Standards/Hypercriticalness: Excessive demands for perfection.
  • Punitiveness: Belief that people deserve harsh punishment for mistakes.

Coping Modes

Schemas trigger coping modes—ways of dealing with the pain. These modes perpetuate the cycle of emotional suffering.

  • Surrender Mode: Giving in to the schema (e.g., feeling defective and accepting it).
  • Avoidance Mode: Avoiding triggers (e.g., detachment, substance use, dissociation).
  • Overcompensation Mode: Acting opposite to the schema (e.g., becoming overly controlling to avoid vulnerability).

By identifying unmet needs, maladaptive schemas, and coping modes, individuals can begin the process of healing. Therapy provides a safe space to reparent the inner child, meet unmet needs, and develop healthier ways of coping.
