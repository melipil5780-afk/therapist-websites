The 5 Essential Emotional Needs

The frustration of core emotional needs, especially during childhood, can lead to the development of negative early maladaptive patterns. These are persistent patterns of negative thoughts, emotions, sensations, and memories that shape how we view ourselves, others, and the world.

1. Secure Attachments to Others: The need for safety, stability, and acceptance.

2. Autonomy & Identity: The need to feel independent and capable.

3. Freedom of Expression: The need to express valid needs and emotions authentically.

4. Spontaneity and Play: The need for creativity, enjoyment, and relaxation.

5. Realistic Limits: The need for boundaries that foster self-regulation.

How Needs Become Negative Patterns

Toxic Frustration: When support is absent, leading to feelings of emptiness.
Traumatization: Abuse creates a belief that others will always harm or neglect you.
Too Much of a Good Thing: Overprotection prevents the development of independence.
Selective Internalization: Absorbing the negative behaviors or victim roles of parents.

The 18 Maladaptive Patterns

Disconnection and Rejection

Family environments that were cold, neglectful, or unpredictable.

1. Abandonment: The belief that support is unreliable.
2. Mistrust/Abuse: The expectation that others will harm or manipulate you.
3. Emotional Deprivation: The belief that nurturance, empathy, or protection will never be met.
4. Defectiveness/Shame: Feeling inherently flawed or unlovable.
5. Social Isolation: A sense of being excluded or different from others.

Impaired Autonomy

6. Dependence: Feeling unable to manage everyday tasks alone.
7. Vulnerability to Harm: Exaggerated fear of imminent catastrophe.
8. Enmeshment: Excessive emotional dependence at the cost of the self.
9. Failure: The belief that one is destined to be inadequate.

Impaired Limits & Other-Directedness

10. Entitlement: Feeling superior or disregarding social norms.
11. Insufficient Self-Control: Difficulty exercising frustration tolerance.
12. Subjugation: Surrendering control to avoid conflict.
13. Self-Sacrifice: Prioritizing others to avoid guilt.
14. Approval-Seeking: Decisions based on external validation rather than the self.

Understanding Coping Modes

A coping mode refers to the strategies we develop to manage or avoid the emotional pain from these patterns. While they provide temporary relief, they don't heal the root.

1. Overcompensation (Fight): Adopting the opposite behavior of the negative belief (e.g., striving for perfection to hide feeling flawed).

2. Avoidance (Flight): Distancing oneself from situations that trigger the pain (e.g., substance use or avoiding intimacy).

3. Surrender (Freeze): Giving in to the pattern and accepting it as true (e.g., choosing partners who repeat the childhood neglect).
